Hop the Shortcut

A site-specific intervention in front of N10.

In front of N10, people repeatedly cut across the grass to get to the convenience store faster. I responded to this invisible routine by placing a hopscotch pattern on the worn path.

The path already existed. My intervention only made its rule visible.

01

Site + Protocol

Site

The site is a patch of ground in front of N10, between the brick path and the entrance area. It sits along the route people use when heading toward the convenience store.

Protocol

Although a brick road already exists, people often walk across the grass because it is faster. Over time, repeated footsteps erased the grass and produced an unofficial path.

Official protocol

Follow the brick road.

Actual protocol

Take the shortcut.

02

Process + Documentation

Idea

A game inserted into a shortcut.

I first imagined a hopscotch pattern placed directly on the desire path. Instead of blocking the shortcut or correcting people’s movement, I wanted to turn the existing movement into a visible game.

Prototype

The logic worked; the material failed.

The prototype confirmed that the format worked. The hopscotch was appropriate to step into, jump over, and play with. However, the craft foam was too light and fragile, and the ground was too stiff for the U-pins to stay in place.

Final Installation

The path became a playable form.

For the final version, I used stronger and clearer hopscotch pieces. The pattern was placed directly over the worn shortcut, following the direction people already used.

03

Observation

After installation, the shortcut became more noticeable. Some people slowed down, looked at the pattern, or adjusted their steps as they passed through the site.

The intervention did not block movement. It changed how the movement was perceived.

I did not change the destination. I changed the way the path was read.
